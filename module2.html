Problem Framing

📖 12 min read🎯 Topic 1 of 16
Why This Matters

Before writing any ML code, you need to clearly define what you're trying to predict and what data you have. A badly framed problem wastes weeks of work. The best ML engineers spend more time framing than coding.

What Makes a Problem an ML Problem?

ML is a good fit when: (1) a pattern exists in the data, (2) you can't write explicit rules for it, (3) you have enough data. Example: recognizing spam emails — you can't list every possible spam pattern, but ML can learn from thousands of examples.

Features vs Labels

Features (X) are the inputs — what you know. Labels (y) are the output — what you want to predict. For house prices: features = square footage, bedrooms, location; label = price.

Train / Validation / Test Split

Think of it like studying for an exam:

  • Training set (70%) — your textbook. The model learns from this.
  • Validation set (15%) — practice exams. Tune your approach without touching the final test.
  • Test set (15%) — the final exam. Only use ONCE to get your real score.

Baselines

Always start with a "dumb" model. For regression: predict the mean. For classification: predict the most common class. If your fancy model can't beat the baseline, something is wrong.

Data Preparation

📖 14 min read🧹 Topic 2 of 16
Why This Matters

Data scientists spend 80% of their time cleaning data. Garbage in = garbage out. No ML algorithm can overcome terrible data quality.

Imputing Missing Values

Mean: good for normally distributed data. Median: robust against outliers. Mode: use for categorical data.

Encoding Categorical Data

Label Encoding: red=0, blue=1, green=2. Simple but implies ordering. One-Hot Encoding: creates binary columns — is_red, is_blue, is_green. No ordering implied.

Scaling

StandardScaler: centers at 0, scales to unit variance. MinMaxScaler: scales to [0, 1]. Many ML algorithms (like SVM, kNN) need features on the same scale to work properly.

Data Leakage & Cross-Validation

📖 10 min read⚠️ Topic 3 of 16
Why This Matters

Data leakage is the #1 reason ML models perform great in testing but fail in production. It's like accidentally seeing the test answers before the exam — your score means nothing.

Common Leakage Sources

  • Scaling before splitting: If you fit a scaler on ALL data, the test set's statistics leak into training
  • Using future data: In time series, accidentally using tomorrow's data to predict today
  • Target leakage: A feature that's derived from the label (e.g., "transaction_approved" when predicting fraud)

Cross-Validation

K-Fold CV splits data into K parts, trains on K-1, tests on the remaining 1, and rotates. This gives you K different scores — much more reliable than a single train/test split.

Linear Regression: Predicting Numbers

📖 14 min read📈 Topic 4 of 16
Why This Matters

Linear regression is the "Hello World" of ML. It's simple, interpretable, and surprisingly powerful. Understanding it gives you the foundation for every other ML algorithm.

The Line of Best Fit

Linear regression finds the best straight line through your data: y = w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + b. The "weights" (w) tell you how much each feature matters. The "bias" (b) is the y-intercept.

Loss Function: Mean Squared Error

MSE measures how far off predictions are from actual values: MSE = (1/n) × Σ(actual - predicted)². The model adjusts weights to minimize this.

Gradient Descent

Gradient descent is how the model learns. Imagine a ball rolling down a bowl — it moves in the direction of steepest descent until it reaches the bottom (minimum error).

Regularization

📖 10 min read🎚️ Topic 5 of 16
Why This Matters

Models can memorize training data instead of learning patterns. Regularization prevents this overfitting — it's the difference between a model that works on paper and one that works in the real world.

Overfitting vs Underfitting

Overfitting: Model memorizes training data, fails on new data. Like memorizing test answers without understanding. Underfitting: Model too simple to capture patterns. Like guessing randomly.

Logistic Regression

📖 12 min read🎲 Topic 7 of 16
Why This Matters

Logistic regression is the foundation of classification — predicting categories instead of numbers. Spam detection, medical diagnosis, sentiment analysis — all classification problems.

From Regression to Classification

Linear regression predicts numbers. But what if you want to predict yes/no, spam/not-spam? You need a function that squashes any number into a probability between 0 and 1. That's the sigmoid function.

The Sigmoid Function

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(-z)) — maps any input to [0, 1]. If output > 0.5, predict class 1. Otherwise, predict class 0.

Decision Trees

📖 12 min read🌳 Topic 8 of 16
Why This Matters

Decision trees are one of the most intuitive ML models — they make decisions the way humans do, through a series of yes/no questions. They're also the building block of powerful ensemble methods like Random Forests.

How It Works

A decision tree asks a series of questions about features to split data into groups. It starts with all data at the root, then repeatedly asks "Is feature X > threshold?" to create branches. The goal: make each resulting group as "pure" as possible (all same class).

Key Concepts

  • Node: A decision point (asks a question)
  • Leaf: A final prediction (no more questions)
  • Entropy: Measures disorder — pure groups have low entropy
  • Information Gain: How much a split reduces entropy

Random Forests

📖 10 min🌲 Topic 9 of 16
Why This Matters

Random Forests combine many decision trees into one powerful model. They're one of the most widely used ML algorithms — accurate, robust, and hard to overfit. Many Kaggle competitions have been won with Random Forests.

Bagging: Many Trees > One Tree

Each tree sees a random subset of data and features. They all vote, and the majority wins. This "wisdom of the crowd" effect makes Random Forests much more robust than single trees.

Key Parameters

  • n_estimators — number of trees (100-500 typical)
  • max_depth — max depth per tree
  • max_features — how many features each tree considers

Classification Metrics

📖 12 min📊 Topic 10 of 16
Why This Matters

Accuracy alone can be misleading. A cancer detector with 99% accuracy sounds great — until you realize 99% of patients are healthy. Understanding precision, recall, and F1 is critical for real-world ML.

The Confusion Matrix

Predicted PositivePredicted Negative
Actually PositiveTrue Positive (TP) ✓False Negative (FN) — missed!
Actually NegativeFalse Positive (FP) — false alarmTrue Negative (TN) ✓

Metrics

  • Precision = TP / (TP + FP) — "of those predicted positive, how many truly are?"
  • Recall = TP / (TP + FN) — "of those actually positive, how many did we catch?"
  • F1-Score = 2 × (P × R) / (P + R) — harmonic mean, balances both

ROC Curve & AUC

📖 10 min📈 Topic 11 of 16
Why This Matters

The ROC curve gives you the complete picture of a classifier's performance across ALL thresholds. AUC boils it down to a single number for easy comparison between models.

ROC = Receiver Operating Characteristic

The ROC curve plots True Positive Rate (Recall) vs False Positive Rate at every possible threshold. A perfect model hugs the top-left corner. A random model is a diagonal line.

AUC = Area Under the Curve

AUC summarizes the ROC curve as a single number. AUC=1.0 = perfect. AUC=0.5 = random guessing. AUC < 0.5 = worse than random (your labels may be flipped).

K-Means Clustering

📖 10 min🎯 Topic 12 of 16
Why This Matters

Sometimes you don't have labels — you just want to find natural groups in data. Customer segmentation, document grouping, anomaly detection all use clustering. K-Means is the most popular algorithm for this.

Unsupervised Learning

Unlike supervised learning (which has labels), clustering finds patterns without being told what to look for. It discovers structure on its own.

K-Means Algorithm

  1. Choose K (number of clusters)
  2. Randomly place K centroids
  3. Assign each point to the nearest centroid
  4. Move each centroid to the center of its assigned points
  5. Repeat steps 3-4 until centroids stop moving

Elbow Method

How do you choose K? Try different values, plot the "inertia" (sum of distances to centroids). The "elbow" — where improvement slows down dramatically — is your K.

PCA (Principal Component Analysis)

📖 10 min🔍 Topic 13 of 16
Why This Matters

Real datasets often have hundreds of features. PCA reduces dimensions while preserving the important information — essential for visualization and speeding up ML algorithms.

The Curse of Dimensionality

More features isn't always better. With too many dimensions, data becomes sparse, distances become meaningless, and models overfit. PCA finds the directions of maximum variance and projects data onto fewer dimensions.

Time Series Basics

📖 10 min⏰ Topic 14 of 16
Why This Matters

Stock prices, temperature readings, website traffic — data with a time dimension requires special handling. You can't shuffle it randomly. Order matters. AI agents monitoring systems deal with time series data constantly.

Components of Time Series

  • Trend: Long-term direction (going up or down)
  • Seasonality: Repeating patterns (sales spike every December)
  • Noise: Random fluctuations

Time-Aware Splits

Never use random splits for time series! Train on past data, test on future data. Otherwise you're using the future to predict the past (data leakage).

Transition to GenAI

📖 8 min🚀 Topic 16 of 16
Why This Matters

You now understand classical ML. The next revolution — Generative AI — builds on these foundations but adds the ability to create, reason, and act. Understanding where ML ends and GenAI begins is key to this course.

What Traditional ML Can and Can't Do

Can DoCan't Do
Classify (spam/not spam)Write a paragraph explaining why
Predict numbers (house prices)Have a conversation about real estate
Cluster similar itemsDescribe the clusters in natural language
Detect anomaliesExplain what the anomaly means

The Neural Network Revolution

Neural networks learn representations instead of requiring hand-crafted features. Give a neural network raw pixels, and it learns edges → shapes → objects. This ability to learn from raw data at scale is what enabled GenAI.

Transfer Learning

Instead of training from scratch, you can take a model trained on millions of examples and fine-tune it for your task. This is why GPT-4 can be adapted for legal, medical, or coding tasks — it transfers knowledge.

Why Language Models Changed Everything

LLMs showed that a model trained on text can: write, translate, code, reason, and even use tools. This was the catalyst for AI agents — systems that can plan, act, and learn. That's what Module 3 is about.
